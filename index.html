Generative Art · Real-time

Real-time Pixel Pipeline

VIS 145A · 2025
About

A node-based generative pipeline built in TouchDesigner, using a source image of Goku from Dragon Ball as input. The project explores what happens when you push pixel manipulation to its structural limits — Displacement, Contrast, UV Weight, and Pre-Black Level all adjusted in real-time to transform a recognisable image into something between signal and noise.

Process

Each parameter in the node network affects the output live, with zero latency. At high contrast and displacement values, the image explodes outward from its centre — the radial burst visible in the output is not a filter but an emergent property of the pixel flow. The result sits somewhere between portrait and abstract: Goku is still legible at the core, but the edges dissolve into pure motion.

Exported at 60fps, the pipeline was designed as a study in visual hierarchy under transformation — how much can you distort an image before recognition collapses?

Research · Psychoacoustics

Auditory Cognition RA

UCSD Lab · 2025–2026
Project

Research assistant on a study investigating how humans recognise and label everyday household sounds — the creak of a door, water running, footsteps on different surfaces. The core question: what acoustic and contextual cues do people rely on when identifying sounds outside of music or speech?

Method

Sounds were recorded using binaural microphones placed at ear-canal level, capturing the spatial and directional qualities that reach the human auditory system. This approach preserves the natural head-related transfer function (HRTF) — the way the shape of the ear and head transforms incoming sound — which is typically lost in standard stereo recordings.

Participants listened to recordings and responded through a custom experiment interface: confidence sliders rating certainty of identification, free-response descriptors (what words come to mind?), and experiential questionnaires probing associated memories, emotions, and contexts.

Contribution

Assisted with experiment design, participant recruitment and scheduling, data collection sessions, and preliminary data cleaning. The study contributes to a growing field examining how the brain builds auditory scene analysis — how we make sense of a world full of overlapping, unlabelled sound.

Installation · Art

Sediments of Memory

VIS 110A · 2026 · Mandeville Gallery, UCSD
Artist Statement

Memory is not simply lost over time; it often settles and lodges itself within the body. Inspired by Bessel van der Kolk's The Body Keeps the Score, this installation reflects on fragmented memories that persist even as others fade — particularly in the context of Alzheimer's disease.

The work is activated when a viewer turns a hand crank. This bodily action gradually illuminates twenty zones of light, translating physical movement into a slow visual unfolding. Each zone is filtered through differently colored materials that act as emotional lenses through which memories are perceived and transformed.

As the light slowly grows and fades, the installation evokes the unstable nature of remembering — moments when memories briefly surface before slipping away again. Through shifting colour and layered illumination, the work suggests that memories are not fixed records but sedimentary traces shaped by time, emotion, and the body.

Personal Context

My grandfather fled North Korea during the Korean War. He raised me. Now he is slowly forgetting me through Alzheimer's. This piece began as an attempt to make that loss felt — not just understood.

About the Work

A film photography series honouring my grandfather — who fled North Korea during the Korean War and raised me. Each object in the series is something I actually used as a child: an 이태리 타올 (Italian exfoliating towel), a 소고 (small drum), a 부채 (folding fan), a 복주머니 (embroidered pouch), a framed photograph of my hometown in Seoul.

These are things I no longer use often, but they hold the texture of a Korean childhood — the smell of bathtime, the sound of a holiday table, the weight of something carried from one country to another. Now, with Alzheimer's taking my grandfather's memory, they have become the last legible language between us.

Visual Language

Each object is photographed against a stark background with intentional glitch effects — chromatic aberration, digital distortion bleeding into analogue film. The glitch is the thesis: memory is not a clean archive. It breaks, shifts, saturates in some places and fades in others. The Korean flag flies above the American one, but the image tears apart at the point where they meet.

Decision Paralysis on Streaming Platforms

COGS 13 · 2025
Research Question

Why does it take so long to choose something to watch? This study investigated decision-making behaviour on streaming platforms through naturalistic observation — watching people actually use Netflix, not asking them to recall it.

Method

20 observation sessions across dormitories and shared living spaces at UCSD. A custom ethogram coded: participant type (solo/group), navigation pattern (linear/circular/random), decision strategy (search/browse/revisit), and post-selection satisfaction.

Sessions ranged from spontaneous viewing to planned movie nights. No intervention — just watching people watch.

Key Findings

Groups averaged 14.7 minutes to make a selection; solo viewers averaged 6.3 minutes. The most striking pattern was circular navigation — returning repeatedly to the same titles without selecting them. This loop revealed a psychological tension between familiarity-seeking and novelty-seeking under perceived choice overload.

Paradoxically, having more options did not increase confidence — it increased paralysis. Participants with the most titles browsed the longest and reported the lowest satisfaction after choosing.

Portable Charger Redesign

DSGN 1 · 2025
Brief

A full end-to-end UX design process applied to a product almost everyone owns but rarely loves: the portable phone charger. The goal was not to redesign for aesthetics, but to identify genuine pain points through user research and address them systematically.

Process

User interviews (8 participants) identified three recurring frustrations: forgetting the cable, not knowing remaining capacity until it dies, and the awkwardness of charging while holding the phone. A survey of 40 respondents confirmed these as the most common issues across age groups.

Design space mapping placed competing solutions across axes of portability vs. functionality. The proposed redesign introduced: magnetic cable attachment (integrated, no loose parts), a real-time LED capacity bar visible without plugging in, and an adjustable grip bracket for comfortable simultaneous phone use.

It All Starts When You Are Here

VIS Studio · 2024
About

A moving-image essay built from film photographs of San Diego's skateboarding culture — the spots, the gaps, the idle hours between runs. Shot on film for the grain, the imprecision, the way analogue slows time down just enough to notice what's really there.

The title is less about skateboarding and more about attention: the particular quality of presence that happens when you commit to a place. Each frame is a moment of someone being entirely where they are.

Form

Slow transitions, ambient sound, minimal editing intervention. The pacing mirrors the rhythm of an afternoon at a skate spot — long stretches of nothing, punctuated by motion. An ode to presence, place, and the people who return to the same concrete again and again.

Close Watch

VIS 70N · 2024
Synopsis

A college party. A string of unsolved murders on the trail near campus. A girl who leaves early because she's too drunk. A guy who insists on walking her home — because there's a killer out there, because he just wants to make sure she's safe.

The film uses the language of care to build dread. Every gesture of concern is also a step closer. The audience is asked to hold two readings simultaneously — and the final line collapses them.

Role & Production

As producer, I coordinated a crew of four across pre-production, shoot logistics, and post-production. The film was made with no budget, shot on location across UCSD's campus and surrounding trails, and edited to sustain a slow, creeping tension rather than rely on conventional horror mechanics.

Theme

The film is interested in how violence hides inside the syntax of protection — how the same words and gestures that signal safety can conceal threat. It does not resolve this ambiguity. It sits in it.

Tide Ink

VIS 141B · 2025
Overview

A live data visualisation rendered across a 4×5 screen wall (20 displays) in the UCSD vis-ramps lab. Real-time tidal data from five California NOAA stations — San Diego, La Jolla, Santa Barbara, San Francisco, Monterey — flows as ink across the entire wall simultaneously.

How It Works

MPI (mpirun) distributes computation across 20 parallel processes — one per screen. Rank 0 fetches NOAA tide predictions every 120 seconds and broadcasts to all ranks. Each rank renders one screen, but the particle flow field is computed in global wall coordinates, so the entire 4×5 surface reads as a single continuous body of water.

Tide height drives particle energy, colour temperature, and flow frequency. Past tides appear cooler and slower; future predictions warmer and more agitated. The ink accumulates on an alpha surface with gentle fade — a sediment effect — rather than clearing between frames.

Technical Details

Four columns map to time slices: PAST · NOW · FUTURE · AVG. Five rows map to five California tide stations. The result is a 4×5 matrix where horizontal position encodes time and vertical position encodes geography — the whole wall becomes a spatial timeline of Pacific coastal tides.

Linguistic Interference

COGS 189 · 2026
Research Question

Does the type of background audio — music with lyrics you understand (MC: Music with Comprehensible lyrics), music with foreign lyrics (MF), or music with domestic lyrics (MD) — affect cognitive load during a working task? The hypothesis: understood lyrics create linguistic interference, measurable through EEG band power.

Method

18 participants, 64-channel EEG at 512 Hz. Three music conditions recorded across multiple trials. Key features extracted: Frontal Beta power (attention/cognitive load), Parietal/Occipital Alpha power (relaxation/inattention), and a Focus Index (Beta/Alpha ratio).

Group-level statistics used repeated-measures ANOVA. Leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) cross-validation tested whether simple bandpower features could classify conditions across participants.

Results

No significant condition effect was found in rmANOVA for Focus Index (p ≈ 0.258) or Alpha Stability (p ≈ 0.212). LOSO classification accuracy was near chance (~0.509), suggesting these features do not reliably generalise across individuals.

This is still a valid scientific result. Null findings in EEG research are meaningful — they suggest that simple cluster-averaged bandpower may not be sensitive enough to capture linguistic interference, and point toward richer features (e.g. temporal dynamics, source-localised signals) for future work.

Predicting Song Popularity

COGS 108 · 2025 · Team Project
Question

Can a song's audio features — tempo, energy, danceability, valence, key, loudness — predict its Spotify Popularity Index? The assumption: if music has measurable acoustic properties that listeners respond to, those properties should correlate with popularity.

Dataset & Method

2,000 songs from Kaggle's Spotify Music Dataset (1998–2020), covering pop, hip-hop, R&B, rock, electronic, latin, and more. Extensive EDA including outlier detection, categorical encoding of musical key and mode, and correlation analysis across all feature pairs.

Finding

Audio features are surprisingly poor predictors of popularity — nearly all R² values near 0. The strongest correlations were loudness (+0.03) and acousticness (+0.02); valence showed a slight negative correlation (-0.02).

The real finding is the question it raises: if acoustic features don't drive popularity, what does? Artist fame, playlist placement, marketing, and algorithmic recommendation all outweigh the sound itself. Popularity is a sociotechnical outcome, not just an acoustic one.
