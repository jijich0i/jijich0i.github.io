Process

Each parameter in the node network affects the output live. At high displacement and contrast, the image kind of explodes outward — that burst shape isn't a filter, it's what the pixel flow does on its own. Exported at 60fps. The question I kept asking: at what point does recognition collapse?

Auditory Cognition RA

UCSD Lab · 2025–2026
What the study was

A study on how people recognize and name everyday sounds — a door creaking, water running, footsteps on different surfaces. The question was what cues people use when they're not hearing music or speech, just regular ambient sounds.

How we recorded

Sounds were recorded using binaural microphones at ear-canal level, which captures the spatial information your ears actually get. Standard stereo doesn't do that — it loses the directional cues that come from the shape of your head and ears (the HRTF). Binaural recording keeps that intact.

Participants listened back through headphones and answered: confidence sliders for how sure they were, free-response for what words came to mind, and questionnaires about memories or emotions the sound triggered.

My role

Helped with experiment design, recruited and scheduled participants, ran data collection sessions, and did preliminary data cleaning.

Sediments of Memory

VIS 110A · 2026 · Mandeville Gallery, UCSD
Artist Statement

Memory doesn't just disappear — it settles in the body. I made this piece thinking about my grandfather, who has Alzheimer's, and about Bessel van der Kolk's idea that the body keeps the score.

When you turn the hand crank, 20 LED zones slowly light up. Each one uses a different colored material to filter the light. The crank is important — it means you have to physically do something to make the memory appear. And then it fades again.

The title comes from the idea that memories are like sediment — they don't disappear cleanly, they layer and compress and shift over time.

Context

My grandfather fled North Korea during the Korean War and raised me. He now has Alzheimer's. I made this piece because I wanted to make that experience felt, not just described.

Decision Paralysis on Streaming Platforms

COGS 13 · 2025
Question

Why does it take so long to pick something to watch? I wanted to actually observe people doing it, not ask them to remember.

Method

20 observation sessions in dorms and shared living spaces at UCSD. I coded what I saw: solo vs. group, how they navigated (linear, circular, random), what strategy they used (search, browse, revisit), and whether they seemed satisfied after choosing. No intervention — just watching.

What I found

Groups took 14.7 minutes on average to pick something. Solo viewers took 6.3 minutes. The most common pattern in groups was circular navigation — going back to the same titles over and over without picking them. Having more options didn't help. If anything, people with more content available took longer and were less happy with what they chose.

Portable Charger Redesign

DSGN 1 · 2025
Brief

Redesign a portable phone charger. Full UX process — research through prototype.

Research

Interviewed 8 people. Ran a survey with 40 respondents. The same three problems came up every time: forgetting the cable, not knowing how much battery is left until it dies, and it being awkward to use while you're still holding your phone.

Mapped the design space against competing products. Most of them solve one problem and ignore the others.

Proposed Design

Magnetic cable that attaches to the charger so you can't forget it. LED bar on the outside showing battery level without needing to plug anything in. Adjustable grip bracket so you can hold phone and charger together without it being awkward.

Tide Ink

VIS 141B · 2025
What it does

Pulls live tidal data from 5 NOAA stations along California — San Diego, La Jolla, Santa Barbara, San Francisco, Monterey — and renders it as flowing particles across a 4×5 screen wall. 20 screens, runs as one piece.

How it works

Used MPI to run one process per screen. Rank 0 fetches new tide data every 120 seconds and sends it to all 20 ranks. Each rank renders its own screen, but the particle flow is computed in global wall coordinates — so the whole wall reads as one surface, not 20 boxes.

Tide height controls particle speed, color temperature, and frequency. Past tides are cooler and slower. Predicted tides are warmer and more active. Particles accumulate and fade slowly instead of resetting — you get a sediment effect over time.

Layout

4 columns = time (past, now, near future, average). 5 rows = 5 stations. The whole wall is a spatial timeline of California coast tides.

Linguistic Interference

COGS 189 · 2026
Question

Does the type of background music affect how well you can focus? Specifically: does music with lyrics you actually understand interfere more than foreign lyrics or instrumental?

Method

18 participants, 64-channel EEG. Three music conditions. I measured frontal beta power (attention) and parietal alpha power (relaxation), then computed a focus index from the ratio. Used repeated-measures ANOVA to compare conditions, and LOSO cross-validation to test if these features could classify conditions across people.

Result

No significant effect (p ≈ 0.258). Classification accuracy near chance (~0.509). So the short answer is: with these features, you can't tell which condition someone was in just from their EEG.

That's still useful. It means simple bandpower probably isn't sensitive enough for this kind of question, and points toward needing richer features — like temporal dynamics or source-localized signals — if you want to actually detect linguistic interference.

Predicting Song Popularity

COGS 108 · 2025 · Team Project
Question

Can you predict how popular a song is just from its audio features — tempo, energy, danceability, valence, loudness?

What we did

2,000 songs from Kaggle (1998–2020), covering a bunch of genres. Did full EDA — outlier detection, encoding musical key and mode, correlation analysis across all feature pairs. Then tried to model popularity from the features.

What we found

Basically nothing. R² near 0 across the board. The strongest correlation was loudness at +0.03. Acousticness was +0.02. Valence was actually slightly negative (-0.02).

Which makes sense when you think about it. Popularity isn't really about what a song sounds like — it's about who made it, where it got placed, how the algorithm treated it. The sound is almost beside the point.
